Hi! My name is [1]Karyn[/1]. I'm originally from Tokyo, now based in New York, usually camouflaging as an artist or an engineer. This is my long ish very honest bio and a sincere attempt at trying to be a bit more legible. If that's not your thing here is the full archive of things I've made.

I liked physics for a long time and went to MIT thinking I would study physics. I accidentally found my way into a creative practice by being really stubborn whenever my teachers would act like there was only one way to do something and spending a lot of time dumpster-diving in the e-waste cage.

While at MIT, I once graffitied a [5]400ft wide, 10 story tall building with light[/5]. I also picked up over 60 screens, CRTs, tons of cables and miscellaneous video gear, unused, half broken computers, and Frankensteined it all together, in a 22 channel video server that drove a [6]20ft tall tower of suspended CRTs in glass tanks filled with oil[/6] all situated in a secret abandoned Frank Gehry bar on campus that I got access to and somehow convinced a bunch of important people to not only approve of all that but fund it as well.

I did a lot of [2]data visualization[/2] work as part of the [10]Civic Data Design Lab[/10] and made a lot of websites and [3]interactive projection work[/3] for myself and for others. I became deeply familiar with the mechanisms of mediation in the context of real-time video. I was also frustrated by the limited opportunities to pursue questions and build things that weren't relevant in academic research and weren't legible enough for industry. Every obsolete gadget I would find in the dumpsters held infinite possible paths that weren't taken. I spent a lot of time in my fav place on campus, the tech closet, where I was constantly tinkering and imagining all these paths. One time in a media theory class I did my very best to try to [11]convince[/11] my professor Caroline Jones that the clean up and organization after an installation was the center of my practice.

Then I moved to New York and worked for an [8]old TV shop in SoHo, taking apart CRTs[/8] for repair and restoration of Nam June Paik pieces while working at a famous Japanese jazz bar in Midtown. At the same time I was showing a lot of my own [6]sculptural work[/6] in the gallery setting. I brought a [7]NY jazz duo to a boutique in Tokyo[/7] for a show, among other [9]solo and group shows[/9] at Tata (Tokyo), Mother's Tank Station (London), lower cavity (Massachusetts), and Foreign & Domestic (New York).

I made a fun [12]trailer for my friend Luke Igel's documentary on MIT history: REGRESSIONS[/12], then I started exploring diffusion models. I became very interested in how AI will come to understand the syntactic logic of the world and made a [8]video piece[/8] questioning, what exactly is a synthetic process that knows how to put together instance to instance and … what does it … look like? The video was featured in NOWNESS.

I decided I wanted to get a lot better at generating images with diffusion so I joined the [13]Human-AI Collaboration Lab[/13] on their research in [15]deepfake identification[/15]. With the lab, I published a paper in CHI and a preprint (with a totally normal not ominous cover graphic) presented at IC2S2 on [19]taxonomizing visual artifacts in AI-generated images as well as identifying people's heuristics for fakeness[/19]. I left the lab to join [14]SITU Research[/14], a visual investigations nonprofit where I did computer vision and forensic tooling research for the analysis of video evidence. The work was presented at the [15]Technical University of Munich[/15] alongside adjacent computational journalism studios like Forensic Architecture.

At SITU I spent a lot of time watching security camera, body camera, footage and was consistently confronted with how when you have a piece of media that is your only way of understanding what really happened in an event, then it becomes really important how that piece of media was produced whether it is where a camera was positioned in the environment, how that camera shoots images, and all the layers of analysis that you can do on top of it. The layers of analysis, then have to be assembled into a narrative and when reality is being tested, it isn't enough to be true, this has to be a compelling narrative.

Upon an invitation from fashion photographer Nick Knight to produce work at [16]SHOWStudio[/16] and during my fellowship with the Steve Jobs Archive, I produced a piece called [17]Surface Tension[/17]. The work revolved around a particular [20]kernel of truth[/20] which was the raw footage from a microscope capturing the process of me using a device called optical tweezers to very manually puppeteer individual neuron cells into dancing around asemic forms and eventually managing to spell out the word THOUGHT. The installation was constructed so that this could only be glimpsed through a chain of lenses that each filtered and molded our perception of the raw footage.

After that I began a practice with Wendi Yan on Object Oriented Knowledge (more soon). I helped design and produce an installation for Audio Technica where a musical performance was recorded live and cut into vinyl all live with an assembly line of 1940s record cutter lathes, then joined production designer LeRoy Bennett on Paul McCartney's tour, built a set of [21] animatronic "trash robots"[/21] and a cobbled together AI beautification machine called the"Enhancer" for New York Fashion Week as a group artists called Weather Service.

Most recently, I built an auto calibration software for a massive 34 projector system inside Pier 57 for Google, [22]DAZED 100[/22] put me on their 2025 list and currently I'm doing my MEng at Cornell (NYC) while working on a new piece called Spitballs which is about the mystical forms of syntax.
